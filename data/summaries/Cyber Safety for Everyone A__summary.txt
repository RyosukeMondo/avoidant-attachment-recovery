## Cyber Safety for Everyone A_

### Overview
This excerpt from *Cyber Safety for Everyone* by JaagoTeens uses a true story of online exploitation and the metaphor of the internet as a “virtual world” to teach digital safety. By drawing explicit parallels between real-world streets and online spaces, the chapter frames the internet as an environment where the same relational threats—predators, manipulation, shame—require analogous protective strategies, boundary-setting, and the courage to seek help.

### Core Concepts
- **Dual citizenship (Real World / Virtual World)**: Individuals navigate two overlapping environments, each demanding awareness of danger and the application of learned safety rules.
- **Illusion of solitude online**: The internet’s quietness tricks users into feeling alone, leading to disinhibited disclosures or complacency about the permanent, public nature of posts.
- **Translating offline safety rules**: Concrete childhood warnings (“don’t take toffees from strangers”) must be consciously adapted to digital equivalents (“never accept gifts from online friends”).
- **Shame-based silence after violation**: The case study of Ashmeet illustrates how sexual image abuse triggers overwhelming humiliation, social harassment, and a prolonged period of isolation before a turning point: speaking to someone.
- **Intergenerational guidance despite skill gaps**: Parents may lack technical expertise, but their life-wisdom about human predators and relational danger is the essential protective factor—involvement matters more than knowing the latest app.

### Avoidant Attachment Relevance
- **The internet as a haven for deactivation strategies**: Dismissive-avoidant individuals are drawn to environments that promise control, distance, and autonomy; online interactions can feel safer than face-to-face intimacy, but this pseudo-safety can bypass typical defenses, making them unexpectedly vulnerable to manipulation (e.g., pressure to send images).
- **Compliant vulnerability vs. authentic consent**: Ashmeet’s initial refusal followed by capitulation under pressure mirrors the fearful-avoidant pattern of surrendering boundaries to preserve the relationship. This faux proximity often results in profound betrayal trauma, reinforcing core beliefs that closeness leads to violation and shame.
- **Post-betrayal isolation as a hyperactivating/deactivating spiral**: After the image spread, Ashmeet endured months of harassment alone—classic avoidant withdrawal mixed with fearful rumination. The shame and external vilification calcify the internal working model: “I am defective, others are rejecting, and reaching out is unsafe.”
- **Help-seeking as a reattachment move**: The pivotal recovery moment—“she spoke about the incident to someone and this person helped her”—is a direct contre to avoidant self-reliance. It models that moving from silent endurance to relational disclosure is the key to ending the cycle of humiliation.
- **Generational wisdom as a symbol of the “earned secure” function**: Parents guiding children on the internet without being tech experts parallels how a therapist need not have lived the client’s exact story; a secure-enough other uses broader relational knowledge to co-regulate and protect. This challenges the avoidant belief that help is only valuable if the helper has superior competence.

### Therapeutic Approaches
- **Psychoeducation through the virtual–real analogy**: Framing the digital world as a parallel universe helps avoidant clients externalize internal patterns—privacy settings can be explored as “boundary hardware,” and viral posts as “unexpected emotional exposure.”
- **IFS (Internal Family Systems) perspective**: The part that sent the photo to preserve connection, the part that felt devastated and hid, and the part that finally spoke up can be differentiated and unblended. The incident becomes a map of inner attachment dynamics.
- **Somatic tracking of safety cues**: Teaching clients to notice the false calm that accompanies online “aloneness” versus the authentic felt-sense of being grounded when boundaries are clear—this helps distinguish dissociation (a common avoidant default) from true regulation.
- **Narrative exposure and re-scripting**: The betrayal story can be re-authored to highlight the adaptive moment of disclosure, transforming a shame narrative into a testament of courage. EMDR could be used to desensitize the social humiliation triggers.
- **Attachment-informed relational repair**: The therapeutic relationship becomes a testing ground where a person can ask for help, experience non-judgmental reception, and learn that vulnerability doesn’t invariably lead to exploitation or rejection.

### Practical Applications
- **Digital/life boundary audit**: Clients map their online behaviors (oversharing, people-pleasing, ghosting) onto attachment deactivation strategies, identifying where they abandon themselves to avoid conflict.
- **“Who helps me?” emergency plan**: Just as children are taught offline safety rules, avoidant adults can create a list of trusted contacts they will turn to before a crisis escalates, practicing the act of reaching out as a skill.
- **Mindset shift: “The internet is a public place” as an internal mantra**: This external rule can be internalized to help fearful-avoidant parts stay vigilant without paranoia—recognizing that intimacy always carries exposure risk, but managed exposure is not the same as annihilation.
- **Somatic micro-practices during online interactions**: Before posting or responding, pause and check heart rate, breathing, muscle tension. If there is a pull to comply against one’s own wishes, activate a “state-change” (stand up, orient to the room) to re-engage the prefrontal cortex.
- **Reparenting imagery**: Visualize the protective adult they needed in childhood walking beside them in the virtual world, quietly prompting: “You don’t have to send anything. You can block. You can tell me what just happened.”

### Key Insights
1. **The internet mirrors avoidant defenses by appearing uncrowded and controllable, yet it is the largest public square on earth**; recovery requires a constant mental correction—“I am not alone here, and my actions have relational consequences.”
2. **Ashmeet’s arc from pressured compliance to devastated isolation to eventually speaking out is a precise map of fearful-avoidant reenactment and repair**: capitulation → shame → withdrawal → relational disclosure as the turning point.
3. **Safety in the virtual world is not a technical skill first; it is a relational skill derived from real-world attachment templates**—the chapter’s insistence on translating “don’t talk to strangers” for the digital age is, at heart, a call to strengthen interpersonal discernment and boundary-setting.
4. **Parents’ life-wisdom matters more than their internet fluency**, which mirrors the therapeutic truth that a clinician doesn’t need to have the client’s exact history to guide them; secure presence matters more than perfect expertise.
5. **The normalization of asking for help—modeled by the chapter’s suspenseful question “Who do you think helped her?”—destigmatizes dependency and reframes reaching out as a mature, protective act**, directly undermining the avoidant ideal of invulnerability.

### Significance for Recovery
For individuals on the avoidant attachment spectrum, digital life often becomes a stage where deactivating strategies are both perfected and shattered. Cyber safety education, as reframed through the lens of this excerpt, is not merely about password strength or privacy settings; it is a backdoor into the work of recognizing one’s worth, asserting boundaries under relational pressure, and daring to break silence after shame. The chapter’s core message—that the same relational dangers exist online and that the same relational courage saves us—validates attachment healing by making the abstract relational patterns tangible, offering a concrete laboratory where avoidant defenses can be noticed, grieved, and gradually replaced with connected protection.
