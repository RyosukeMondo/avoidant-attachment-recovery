## EMDR and Attachment-Focused_

### Overview
This book presents Attachment-Focused Trauma Therapy for Adults (AFTT-A), an integrated model that combines attachment theory, parts work (internal personality reconstruction), and EMDR therapy. It offers a structured, phase-based approach to heal the enduring effects of childhood attachment trauma by reorganizing the internal personality system and building an Authentic Self capable of secure relating.

### Core Concepts
- **Attachment-Focused Trauma Therapy for Adults (AFTT-A)**: A preparation-phase model that reconstructs the internal personality system before trauma reprocessing with EMDR.
- **Adaptive Information Processing (AIP) Model**: Early attachment disruptions leave unprocessed, dysfunctionally stored memories that distort present-day internal (parts of Self) and external relationships.
- **Reconstruction of the Internal Personality System**: Identifies and transforms overdeveloped, underdeveloped, blended, and cut-off parts of Self to build a coherent Authentic Self.
- **Authentic Self and Competent Adult**: The goal is to strengthen an Authentic Self and install a Competent Adult part that orchestrates the internal system and present-day life, replacing maladaptive coping roles from childhood.
- **Parallel Process**: Therapists must explore their own attachment histories and internal systems to provide mindful, secure-based attunement and an emotionally corrective therapeutic relationship.
- **Integrating Attachment and Trauma**: Nonsecure and disorganized attachment patterns (present in 30–40% of the nonclinical population) are rooted in adverse childhood experiences like rejection and invalidation, and they drive lifelong emotional and relationship instability without intervention.

### Avoidant Attachment Relevance
- **Targets Deactivating Strategies**: The parts work directly addresses dismissive and fearful-avoidant patterns by identifying “cut-off” parts (suppressed attachment needs, vulnerability) and overdeveloped “protector” parts (hyper-independence, emotional numbing) that maintain avoidance of intimacy.
- **Reclaims Disowned Vulnerability**: For fearful-avoidant individuals who oscillate between craving closeness and fearing it, the model clarifies the internal conflict between yearning parts and defensive parts, allowing integration rather than cycling.
- **Repairs Internal Attachment**: Nonsecure external attachments are mirrored internally; healing the relationship between parts of Self (e.g., a critical inner parent and a wounded child) rewires the template for present-day relationships, enabling earned security.
- **Corrective Therapeutic Relationship**: The therapist’s secure, attuned stance directly challenges the avoidant client’s expectation of rejection, providing a powerful relational antidote to early experiences of insensitive or intrusive caregiving.
- **Neural Network Transformation**: The AIP model explains that avoidant attachment schemas (“others cannot be trusted,” “needs will be punished”) are linked to unprocessed memory networks; bilateral stimulation during parts work deepens positive shifts that undermine these core beliefs.

### Therapeutic Approaches
- **EMDR Therapy Integration**: Uses bilateral stimulation (eye movements, taps, tones) not only for trauma reprocessing but also to deepen positive affective shifts achieved through AFTT-A protocols in the preparation phase.
- **Resource Development and Installation (RDI)**: Builds a Safe/Calm Place and inner resources (e.g., nurturing figures, protector figures) to stabilize the client before accessing traumatic material.
- **Parts Work Informed by Transactional Analysis and Inner Child Models**: Draws on Berne’s Parent-Adult-Child diagrams and Bradshaw’s Inner Child healing to map, dialogue with, and transform internal parts.
- **Phase-Based Treatment Sequence**: Clients first learn emotional regulation and relationship skills, then reconstruct the internal system, and only then proceed to reprocess traumatic memories in EMDR phases 3–8.
- **Therapist Self-Reflection Protocols**: Guided assignments help clinicians notice their own attachment-based reactions, enabling intentional, secure-based responses that model a Competent Adult.
- **Applicability to Dissociative Identity Disorder (DID)**: With adaptations, the protocols can improve internal system health in DID clients before trauma work.

### Practical Applications
- **Mapping Internal Parts**: Clients identify overdeveloped “coping” parts (e.g., the relentless striver, the distancer) and underdeveloped vulnerable parts (e.g., the lonely child), creating a visual or experiential map of their inner architecture.
- **Transforming Blended and Cut-Off Parts**: Through structured protocols and bilateral stimulation, clients soften rigid defenses, release cut-off emotions, and foster compassion between formerly polarized internal parts.
- **Installing an Internal Team**: The Competent Adult part learns to coordinate the newly harmonized inner parts, much like a secure leader managing a team, to navigate stressors and relationships adaptively.
- **Strengthening the Authentic Self**: Regular practice of stepping into a felt sense of the Authentic Self—calm, curious, connected—uses bilateral stimulation to install this state as a stable neural network.
- **Daily Self-Regulation Tools**: Skills from Dialectical Behavioral Therapy (DBT) and Radically Open DBT (RO DBT), referenced by the authors, complement the model by enhancing emotional regulation and flexible, prosocial responding in real-time interactions.
- **Therapist’s Own Internal Work**: Clinicians are encouraged to complete the AFTT-A steps themselves, improving their attunement and preventing their own attachment patterns from derailing a client’s healing.

### Key Insights
1. **Attachment wounds are encoded in the internal personality system**—parts of Self become over- or underdeveloped as survival adaptations, and these inner attachment patterns directly replicate external relationship difficulties.
2. **EMDR’s bilateral stimulation can catalyze the positive restructuring of internal parts**, transforming cut-off protector parts into more flexible, collaborative inner allies, which is especially powerful during the preparation phase.
3. **The Competent Adult part** is the linchpin for lasting change; when installed, it provides the executive functioning and secure-base behavior the client never received from caregivers, enabling completely new relational outcomes.
4. **Therapeutic progress hinges on the parallel process**—the therapist’s unresolved attachment patterns will be mirrored in the client, so clinicians must cultivate their own internal security to become a trusted attachment figure.
5. **Healing avoidant attachment requires an approach that sequentially combines skills, resource development, parts integration, and trauma reprocessing**, rather than jumping prematurely into memory work, as the overdeveloped defensive parts will otherwise block access to core wounds.

### Significance for Recovery
For those with dismissive or fearful-avoidant attachment, this model directly challenges the deeply conditioned belief that safety lies in extreme self-reliance or emotional suppression. By methodically rehabilitating the internal relationship with the vulnerable Self and creating a secure inner leadership, the AFTT-A approach enables a profound shift from isolated vigilance to the capacity for genuine, earned secure connections—a lifeline often missed when avoidant strategies are bypassed rather than restructured.
