## Alone together

### Overview
This autobiographical novel by Fanny D. explores intergenerational trauma, profound attachment wounds, and the torment of feeling fundamentally unwanted. The excerpt centers on a suicidal crisis in which the narrator, Agnès, dissociates from her body and relationships, convinced that her disappearance will finally bring peace to those she loves.

### Core Concepts
- Deep-seated belief of being an unworthy, unwanted child (maternal ambivalence about pregnancy echoed in the narrator’s own abortion)
- Dissociation as a primary defense against overwhelming physical and emotional pain
- The “alone together” paradox — experiencing profound disconnection even when surrounded by others who care
- Attachment-related suicidal ideation where death is framed as liberation for oneself and relief for others
- Intergenerational trauma patterns: maternal unavailability, replaced siblings, and repeated relational ruptures
- The body as a battleground: somatic numbing and lethargy in the face of threat, alternating with moments of intense emotional suffering

### Avoidant Attachment Relevance
- **Dismissive-avoidant features:** The narrator minimizes distress, refuses help, and maintains a mask of imperturbability while facing extreme danger; she actively deactivates attachment needs, insisting that no one truly understands or can reach her.
- **Fearful-avoidant (disorganized) features:** She oscillates between desperate longing for connection (calling Alex repeatedly, arranging a last meeting) and terrified withdrawal (sabotaging rescue, planning suicide). Her inner world is marked by unresolved trauma and contradictory views of self and others.
- **Hyperactivation followed by deactivation:** The pattern of reaching out to Alex and then interpreting his past unavailability as proof that any new promise is meaningless mirrors the push-pull dynamic common in fearful-avoidant strategies.
- **Defensive self-reliance:** Even as she contemplates suicide, she frames it as an autonomous act that will free her mother and Alex to be happy — a distorted expression of the avoidant belief that one must handle everything alone.
- **Blocked mourning and unprocessed loss:** The narrator’s affect is flattened; she describes herself as “atone, très loin de mon corps” (expressionless, far from her body), indicating a dissociative barrier to grieving early attachment losses.

### Therapeutic Approaches
- **Trauma-focused stabilization (phase-oriented treatment):** Before any exposure work, establishing safety and psychoeducation about the window of tolerance is essential, as the narrator shows severe dysregulation and suicidal intent.
- **Somatic interventions:** Given the profound dissociation and numbness, body-based approaches (sensorimotor psychotherapy, somatic experiencing) would help reconnect her to bodily signals without overwhelm.
- **Internal Family Systems (IFS):** The text reveals polarized inner parts — a defiant protector, a wounded exiled child who believes she doesn’t belong, and a suicidal firefighter — all accessible through an IFS lens.
- **Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT):** For the relational wounding, exploring the attachment cycle between Agnès, her mother, and Alex can restructure the insecure bond and address the core fear of being a burden.
- **Cognitive restructuring of core schemas:** Addressing beliefs such as “I am a mistake,” “I cause others’ suffering,” and “I am unlovable” would be a central cognitive target, possibly within a CBT or schema therapy framework.
- **Crisis intervention for suicidal ideation:** Developing a delayed action plan, increasing connectedness, and working with ambivalence (the part that chose to go home first, thus delaying the attempt) are immediate life-saving priorities.

### Practical Applications
- **Track dissociative states:** Learn to recognize the “far from my body” experience early and use grounding techniques (e.g., orienting to the present environment, deliberate breath) to interrupt the dissociative cascade.
- **Map the push-pull cycle:** Create a personal diagram of how reaching out is followed by self-protective withdrawal, and practice naming the fear that fuels each shift without acting on it.
- **Externalize the inner critic:** Give voice to the part that insists you are a burden, writing out its statements, then exploring their origins in early attachment messages rather than objective truth.
- **Delay and connect:** When suicidal thoughts emerge, build a structured delay protocol that includes contacting a safe person, even if the avoidant part insists no one will answer — testing new behavior is key.
- **Grieve the longed-for mother:** Use expressive writing or imagery rescripting to honor the pain of never feeling wanted, beginning the process of separating past rejection from present relationships.
- **Micro-experiments in trust:** Identify small, low-risk opportunities to share a need or feeling with someone safe and observe the outcome, accumulating corrective emotional experiences.

### Key Insights
1. **Self-perceived burden is a relational death sentence.** Agnès interprets her very existence as harmful to those she loves, a core attachment injury that drives suicidal despair and blocks help-seeking.
2. **Dissociation silences the body but preserves the pain.** Her detachment from physical sensation during the assault (and later on the platform) is a survival adaptation that, over time, disconnects her from life itself.
3. **Unanswered calls become attachment proof.** The fact that Alex did not answer her earlier distress calls is internalized as evidence of her unworthiness, reinforcing the avoidant strategy of never counting on anyone.
4. **The “alone together” bind defines disorganized attachment.** Simultaneous hunger for and terror of closeness keeps Agnès in a state of chronic isolation even when relationships are physically present.
5. **Suicidal crisis as a last act of control.** For someone who has experienced repeated helplessness, controlling the time and manner of death feels like the only remaining autonomy — a tragic illusion that must be met with compassionate curiosity, not judgment.

### Significance for Recovery
This excerpt powerfully illustrates how early experiences of being unwanted shape a lifelong narrative of being a wrongness that must be erased. For avoidant attachment recovery, it underscores the need to address the suicidal voice not as enemy pathology but as a distorted protector born from attachment terror — and to offer corrective relational experiences where presence is consistent, safe, and unflinching even when the client pulls away. Healing demands rebuilding the right to belong, one painful, contradictory moment at a time.
