## Force Animal Drawing Animal_

### Overview
This book is a manual for animators that reveals how to see and draw the “FORCE” behind every living creature’s movement. Its rhythmic, anatomical, and design principles offer a profound metaphor for understanding the flow of authentic emotional life and the ways avoidant attachment patterns disconnect a person from their own internal force, spontaneity, and relational momentum.

### Core Concepts
- **FORCE as the fundamental life energy that drives all motion, posture, and expression.** The book teaches that beneath every pose is a directional, dynamic push or pull — a current of intention that must be felt and drawn, not just copied.  
Psychologically, this parallels the core emotional drive system (seeking, play, bonding) that gets suppressed in avoidant attachment.
- **Man-to-animal structural comparison** reveals how shared anatomy achieves different functional shapes. The comparative study of scapula movement, ribcage tilt, and limb architecture maps onto the way human beings share a core biological blueprint for connection but develop characterological “locomotion” patterns through early adaptation.
- **Three locomotor grades (plantigrade, digitigrade, unguligrade)** define how weight is borne, speed is generated, and ground contact is managed — a spectrum from full-footed stability to tiptoe agility. These mirror attachment strategies: the grounded, the semi-distanced, and the highly deactivated or “floating above” relational contact.
- **The Law of Thirds** introduces an abstract proportional system that creates visual balance through asymmetrical thirds. Relationally, this reflects the healthy dance of intimacy and autonomy, closeness and space, that avoidant systems struggle to calibrate.
- **Rhythm and flowing, continuous line** are prioritized over stiff outlines. The animator learns to see a unified curve of energy traveling through the spine, limbs, and tail. This trains perception of an uninterrupted life current — something truncated in the avoidant’s fragmented experience of self and others.

### Avoidant Attachment Relevance
- **Dismissive-avoidant patterns:** The deactivation of attachment needs is like a living body that has lost its visible momentum — the animal is present but drawn as a rigid statue. People with this style often hold themselves in fixed, “loaded” poses (shoulders braced, breathing shallow) that interrupt the natural flow of relational FORCE. The book’s emphasis on seeing through the still frame to the ongoing directional thrust directly addresses this bodily armoring and emotional immobility.
- **Fearful-avoidant patterns:** The chaotic approach-withdrawal dance resembles an animal caught between flight and fawn, with conflicting forces pulling the spine and limbs in opposing directions. The animator’s skill of finding the dominant FORCE among many — and identifying the path of resolution — models how a disorganized internal system can learn to sequence and express competing impulses with clarity rather than collapse.
- **Mechanisms of avoidance as a break in the rhythm line:** Avoidance interrupts the smooth transmission of interpersonal energy, creating abrupt changes in direction that are jarring for the other person. The artist’s eye, trained to detect “broken” or unsupported force paths, would instantly recognize the relational dysrhythmia that follows a deactivating strategy. Recovering the ability to trace one’s own emotional force back to its source (a need, a fear, a desire) is the central healing task.
- **The scapula as a gateway to expressive reach:** The book devotes significant attention to the shoulder blade’s movement as the pivot for limb action. In attachment terms, the shoulder girdle is where reaching out or withdrawing begins. Avoidant individuals often limit the expressive range of their arms and hands, embodying the message “I don’t need you.” Restoring full, unguarded reach — literally and figuratively — undoes the core tension of dismissive self-sufficiency.
- **The “Big Reveal” of shared animal nature** (the skeleton comparison chapter) directly counters the shame-based defensive superiority that often underlies dismissive avoidance. Recognizing that we are all animals, governed by the same visceral forces of hunger, fear, tenderness, and play, dissolves the narrative of separation that makes intimacy feel so threatening.

### Therapeutic Approaches
- **Somatic tracking of directional FORCE:** Borrowing directly from the animator’s process, clients can be guided to draw the “line of force” they feel in their bodies during different emotional states. This externalizes the internal, giving a safe visual language to sensations that dismissive individuals habitually ignore or minimize — an intervention aligned with somatic experiencing and body-based trauma work.
- **Internal Family Systems (IFS) “animator’s perspective”:** Parts are observed as distinct force configurations — a protector that pulls the spine back, an exile that collapses the ribcage, a manager that stiffens the neck. Asking “what is the direction of this part’s force?” helps unblend with cognitive content and access the pure somatic signature of each protective response, reducing the shame that drives avoidance.
- **Rhythmic relational regulation:** The book’s lesson that all motion is continuous and never truly stops can be adapted into co-regulation exercises where therapist and client move together (e.g., walking, breathing, simple hand gestures) while keeping a shared rhythm. This retrains the nervous system’s expectation that connection is a reciprocal, unbroken flow, countering early experiences of misattuned, intrusive, or absent rhythms. This resonates with Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT) and dyadic regulation work.
- **“Counting forces” as a mindfulness intervention:** The book instructs artists to identify how many separate force vectors are at play in a pose. Clinician and client can similarly “count forces” in a charged relational moment — “I notice a force pulling you toward me right now, and another force pulling you back. Are there more?” This mentalizing technique makes ambivalence explicit and workable, particularly for fearful-avoidant indecision.
- **Law of Thirds as a psychoeducational metaphor:** Introducing the proportional principle that dynamic stability comes from asymmetry — not rigid 50/50 balance — helps avoidant clients challenge the all-or-nothing belief that they must either merge and lose themselves or withdraw entirely. Healthy relationships are lived in the third that bridges closeness and distance.

### Practical Applications
- **Drawing your “inner animal” in a moment of stress:** Without any artistic skill required, the client sketches the shape and force lines of what their body feels like right now. A dismissive state might emerge as a heavy, rooted plantigrade refusing to budge; a fearful state as a digitigrade poised for flight but frozen. The drawing becomes a target for curiosity and gentle adjustment — “Where would you add a softer curve?”
- **Walking practices in three locomotor modes:** Experimenting with walking full-footed (plantigrade), on the balls of the feet (digitigrade-like), or with exaggerated heel lift (unguligrade-like) helps clients feel how different ground contact strategies affect their sense of safety and emotional availability. Many avoidant individuals discover they habitually move in a semi-digitigrade, “ready to leave” pattern that maintains distance. Practicing intentional, grounded plantigrade walking can build ventral vagal safety.
- **The FORCE scan meditation:** As a daily regulation practice, slowly trace a continuous line of attention from the tailbone up the spine, through the heart, out the arms, and back — mimicking the animator’s single flowing stroke. This trains interoception, counteracts the fracture of bodily awareness typical in trauma, and reinforces the felt sense of being an integrated, moving whole.
- **Relational rhythm mirroring:** During social interactions, consciously attune to the other’s pace, breathing rhythm, and gesture cadence, then gently match it, as if drawing a shared line of force. Avoidant attachment often prevents this natural mirroring; deliberate, low-stakes practice rebuilds the neurobiological substrate of empathy and rapport without requiring elaborate emotional disclosure.
- **“Skeletal comparison” journaling:** Regularly ask, “What is the structural similarity between my emotional reaction and an animal instinct?” For example, “My impulse to leave the room when my partner shares something deep is structurally the same as a deer suddenly sensing danger and bolting. The instinct itself is not shameful; it’s a force I can learn to ride differently.” This cognitive reappraisal reduces secondary shame and builds self-compassion.

### Key Insights
1. **All life is a transmission of force, and deadness is not the absence of force but its blockage.** Avoidant deactivation is not an absence of attachment need; it is a dammed-up longing that strains the whole structure. Recovery is the art of restoring flow, not creating something new.
2. **The comparison between human and animal anatomy reveals that form follows force.** Our relational shapes — hyper-independent, dismissive, hovering — are the consequence of learned force maneuvers to maintain safety. Changing the form requires first sensing and redirecting the underlying force, not simply adopting new behavioral poses.
3. **Rhythm is the felt quality of secure connection; a break in rhythm is the earliest signal of attachment disruption.** The animator’s attunement to continuous line teaches a transferable sensitivity: avoidance first shows up as a micro-stutter in the relational rhythm, long before words or actions clarify what is happening.
4. **The “Big Reveal” is that we are no different from the animals we draw, and that shared animality is the gateway to compassion, not a cause for contempt.** By reclaiming the body’s animal wisdom, the avoidant person can stop treating dependency as a despised weakness and start honoring it as a lawful biological force.
5. **The Law of Thirds mandates that dynamic balance is never static.** Secure attachment is not a fixed point of perfect closeness; it is an ongoing, oscillating negotiation of space and touch, approaching and retreating in a larger holding rhythm. Mastery lies in tolerating the asymmetry without collapsing into either pole.

### Significance for Recovery
This work matters for avoidant attachment recovery because it bypasses the verbal, analytical fortress that so often protects the dismissive and confused-avoidant heart, speaking directly to the body’s deep grammar of movement, tension, and release. By learning to see and draw the FORCE within themselves, recovering individuals can slowly reclaim the embodied, flowing, and connected animal they never stopped being — transforming shameful isolation into rhythmic belonging.
