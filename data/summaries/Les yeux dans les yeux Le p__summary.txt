### Overview
In this excerpt from Sherry Turkle’s *Les yeux dans les yeux: Le pouvoir de la conversation à l’heure du numérique*, she argues that constant digital connection erodes our capacity for deep, empathetic conversation. Using a seminar in which students confess to texting during deeply personal disclosures, she exposes the myth of multitasking, the loss of sustained attention, and the subtle moral injury caused when we fail to be fully present with one another.

### Core Concepts
- **Attention as a moral signal**: Where we direct our attention shows others what we value; split attention during vulnerable sharing devalues the speaker and erodes trust.
- **The paradox of presence**: Students report needing to be partially absent (checking devices) in order to feel present—a state in which they are never truly available to others or to themselves.
- **The myth of multitasking**: Rapid task-switching produces a dopamine-fueled illusion of efficiency while degrading performance, organisational capacity, and the ability to read emotional cues.
- **Flight from conversation**: Digital connection offers a reassuring, controllable alternative to the unpredictability and demands of face-to-face dialogue, making genuine conversation feel slower, riskier, and more “boring.”
- **Technological sedation of discomfort**: Devices are used to banish boredom, stillness, and the discomfort of bearing witness to others’ pain—emotions that are precisely what deepen relational attunement.
- **Empathy under threat**: Normalising device use alongside someone in tears reflects a desensitisation that Turkle sees as an imminent cultural threshold, with devices becoming emotional shields against other people’s vulnerability.

### Avoidant Attachment Relevance
- **Distraction as deactivation strategy**: Reaching for a phone when conversation becomes emotionally charged mirrors dismissive-avoidant deactivation—using external stimuli to blunt internal and relational distress, maintaining distance from intimacy without appearing to flee.
- **Illusion of control and self-sufficiency**: The multitasking myth and the compulsion to check “qui veut me parler” (who wants to talk to me) provide a sense of mastery and validation that mimics avoidant pseudo-autonomy; real connection is traded for a curated, low-risk social reassurance.
- **Fear of emotional engulfment**: Fearful-avoidant individuals may oscillate between craving connection and fearing it. Partial digital presence allows a semblance of belonging while keeping the depth of others’ painful stories—stories of abuse, abandonment—at a manageable distance, protecting against the overwhelm that can trigger their own unprocessed trauma.
- **Intolerance of stillness**: Avoidantly attached people often experience quiet, unstimulated moments as threatening. Turkle’s students cannot tolerate three minutes without a message; this maps onto the avoidant’s difficulty resting in the present with another person, where they might confront their own emotional states or attachment needs.
- **Moral self-protection and guilt**: The students’ guilt about texting during intimate disclosures reveals a conscience that knows they are violating the relational space. For avoidant individuals, this guilt may be bypassed through rationalisation (e.g., “I can still listen”), but the excerpt unmasks how that rationalisation is a defence against the vulnerability required to be fully receptive.
- **Desensitisation to others’ distress**: Turkle’s prediction—that it will soon feel natural to scroll the phone next to someone weeping—describes a dissociative-like numbing to empathic distress, a hallmark of attachment trauma that keeps the avoidant person at a safe emotional remove from suffering they find unmanageable.

### Therapeutic Approaches
- **Digital-physical frame-setting**: The class experiment of switching off all phones except during a dedicated break functions as a behavioural boundary intervention, creating a secure relational container where full attention is protected and anxiety about missing out is contained.
- **Group processing and consciousness-raising**: Facilitating open discussion about distraction and its moral dimensions (guilt, shame, the sense that something precious is being damaged) helps participants confront avoidance patterns in a supportive group—an approach echoing group therapy and EFT-informed psychoeducation.
- **Psychoeducation on attention and empathy**: Debunking the multitasking myth and teaching the neurobiological costs of split attention (dopamine loops, empathic erosion) empowers clients to reframe their device use not as a harmless habit but as a defence against intimacy.
- **Reframing “boredom” as therapeutic space**: Working with avoidant individuals to tolerate and explore the emptiness that digital stimuli cover—through somatic tracking, parts work, or mindfulness—opens access to unfelt emotions and attachment longings that boredom defends against.
- **Somatic and experiential work**: Because face-to-face presence can dysregulate traumatised or avoidant nervous systems, somatic interventions (grounding, orienting, co-regulation exercises) can expand the window of tolerance for the intensity of undivided eye contact and shared vulnerability.
- **Unblending from the “multitasking protector” part (IFS)**: Identifying the part that reaches for the phone as a protector guarding against fears of engulfment, inadequacy, or emotional demand allows the client to build a compassionate relationship with that protector while leading from Self-energy to risk deeper connection.

### Practical Applications
- **Tech-free conversation covenants**: Establish agreements (in classrooms, therapy groups, intimate relationships) to turn devices off and put them out of sight during moments of emotional sharing; treat this as a deliberate practice of “attention training.”
- **Micro-practice of the three-minute rule**: Challenge the impulse to check a phone during any pause by waiting three minutes while noticing bodily sensations, breathing, and the content of the interaction; use the discomfort as a signal of an avoidant pattern activating.
- **Tracking technology-driven deactivation**: Keep a log of moments when the urge to look at the phone arises during a conversation, noting what was just said and what emotion it provoked—this builds insight into the specific relational cues that trigger withdrawal.
- **Cultivating “unitasking” as intimacy practice**: Schedule daily conversation periods (e.g., meals, walks) where only one activity—talking and listening—is permitted; treat these as exposure exercises for those who fear losing themselves in the presence of another.
- **Using the phone as an intentional signal**: When the impulse to check comes, pause and ask, “What would staying fully present right now communicate to this person about their worth and mine?” Turn the decision into a conscious moral choice rather than a compulsive reflex.
- **Nervous system regulation before connection**: Before entering an important conversation, engage in grounding (feet on the floor, slow exhales) to lower sympathetic arousal, reducing the need to escape into the digital “safe harbour” when emotions intensify.

### Key Insights
1. **We are learning to not be there with each other.** Students feel they can only be present if they are partly absent, revealing a culturally reinforced attachment disturbance where relational presence becomes intolerable without a digital safety valve.
2. **Attention is a primary regulator of relational safety.** When attention is fragmented, the speaker receives a clear message: your story does not matter enough to hold my mind. This undermines the secure base that conversation can provide.
3. **The myth of multitasking is a dopamine delusion that shields the avoidant mind.** It preserves a self-image of efficiency while masking the growing inability to read emotional cues and sustain empathetic resonance—core features of attachment security.
4. **The guilt students feel is a compass pointing home.** Their moral discomfort signals that deep inside, they know what secure connection demands, and their guilt can be mobilised as a therapeutic ally rather than shamed into silence.
5. **Simple constraints restore humanity.** The single act of turning off phones transformed the seminar from distracted, disjointed sharing into a coherent, relaxed, and deeply attentive community—proof that reclaiming conversation is immediately available once the technological barrier is removed.

### Significance for Recovery
For those working to heal dismissive or fearful-avoidant attachment patterns, Turkle’s observations make visible how everyday technological habits reinforce core defensive structures: digital distraction mimics deactivation, partial presence mimics emotional safety, and the myth of multitasking justifies the flight from vulnerability. Recovery requires not only insight but a deliberate reclamation of fully attentive, device-free conversation—a practice that directly challenges the avoidant system by proving that genuine presence is not dangerous, but deeply regulating. In reclaiming conversation, avoidant individuals can rediscover that undivided attention is not a threat to autonomy but the very medium in which secure attachment is built and repaired.
